Asian Lore

Penanggalan

The Penanggalan is a Malaysian vampire-type monster, who separates at the neck and flies with her entrails dragging behind her. During the day she appears as a regular woman, but her head flies off at night so she can flit around terrifying people, and supposedly eats newborn babies. Rituals for protection against the monster are by pregnant women or women who recently gave birth. According to the tale, the Penanggalan smells of vinegar, because she must clean her dangling entrails with it. She does this in order to stuff them back into her body through the neck. Legends claim she was once a normal woman, that is until someone startled her so badly that her head popped off. The monster is called by other names, such as Hantu Penanggal, Leyak, or Krasue, in other Southeast Asian countries.

Mongolian Death Worm

The Mongolilan Death Worm is two to five feet long and spits acid at anyone who crosses its path. Locals in Mongolia's Gobi Desert wholeheartedly believe in the creature's existence. The worm is described to be a large red warm with fangs and suction- like face. If you touch it, or if it spits at you, you will die. This can explain why there are no photographs. Regardless, the lack of photo evidence does not diminish the locals' belief in the creature.

Namahage

The Namahage are Japanese ogres who, legend says, once terrorized the countryside if they weren't given bribes of food and, once a year, a young woman. Namahage are frightful demon-like yōkai which live in the mountains along the northern coast of the Sea of Japan. They look like oni, with bright red or blue skin, wild hair and eyes, large mouths full of sharp teeth, and often have horns sprouting from their forehead. They wear straw leggings and raincoats, and carry large blades. The legend originates as ritual that takes place in Japan on New Year's Eve. From then on people dress in masks as Namahage and go door-to-door scaring children into hard work and good behavior.

Kappa

The Kappa lives in the rivers and waterways of Japan. It is bigger than a turtle, but has a turtle shell or at least scaly skin like a fish. The Kappa is said to be able to walk upright like a human, and it always has a depression in its skull where it keeps water,m. Water is the source of its power. The Kappa comes out of rivers and lakes to enchant children and lure them into the water where it can eat them. This story is often told to children to scare them from getting too close to the water.

Almasty

The Almasty roams the Caucasus mountains of central Asia. The creature has a flat face like that of a human being, and it often walks on two legs. Its body is covered with a thick black fur, and its feet armed with enormous claws. The creature has incredible strength. That is why hunters are afraid of attacking it, and the previous inhabitants removed their homes from the areas the creature was spotted. All in order to keep it from counter attacking.

Nue

The nue is thought to have started appearing in the late Heian period. It’s appearance may be a combination of the animals. It can be mix between a northeast Tiger, a southeast Snake, a southwest Monkey, and a northwest Qian (Dog and Wild Boar). Originally, the nue were stated to be a bird that resembles the green pheasant. Yet their precise identity is unknown, and perhaps can be a shapeshifting beast.

Vetala

Some of the world’s oldest tales of vampires come from India, where ancient Hindu stories were taken to other nations by traders and nomads. Among these beings are the Vetala, who are dead but not at rest because the proper funeral rites were not performed for them. They are also described as evil spirits that occupy corpses. A Vetal has uncanny knowledge of the past, present, and future which it uses to confuse humans. However, there is lore that states that sometimes these vampires become guards or helpers to sorcerers who enslave them. Vetala live in cemeteries, but wander afield to kill children and livestock. A Vetal can be recognized because the corpse’s hands and feet are turned backwards.

Phaya Naga

The Phaya Naga is a Laotian dragon that lives in the Mekong River. It is also known in Thailand, Cambodia, Burma, and Vietnam. The Naga is a benign deity that protects the city of Vientiane. Naga Fireballs are said to be produced by the dragon, but are actually thought to be the product of fermentation below the surface of the water. Thai Folklore holds the Phaya Naga to be semi-divine, demi-creatures, which possess supernatural powers as has been described in Buddhist and Hindu cosmology. The Naga is often reported to live within the enchanted Kamchanod Forest

Tikbalang

The Tikbalang is the demon horse of the Philippines. This monster has the head and feet of a horse with very long limbs, and the body of a human, therefore it walks upright. Tikbalangs or Tigbolan scare travelers, lead them astray and play tricks on them such as making them go in circles.The tikbalang s a ghost which assumes a variety of forms, and sometimes confers a similar gift upon a certain favored individual. A superstition popular with the Tagalogs of Rizal Province is that Tikbalangs are benevolent guardians of elemental kingdoms. They are usually found standing at the foot of large trees looking around for anyone who dares to bestow malignancy on their kingdom's territory. Encounters with the creature can be counteracted by wearing one's shirt inside out. Another countermeasure is to ask permission out loud to pass by. Another tip is to not to make too much noise while in the woods in order not to offend or disturb the tikbalang.

Manticore

The Manticore is a man-eating chimera with the body of a lion and a human head. The legend of the Manticore arises from the Middle East. The Persians just called it "man-eater". The manticore or mantichore is a Persian legendary creature similar to the Egyptian sphinx that proliferated in western European medieval art as well. It has the head of a human, the body of a lion and a tail of venomous spines similar to porcupine quills, while other depictions have it with the tail of a scorpion. There are some accounts that the spines can be shot like arrows, thus making the manticore a lethal predator. It devours its prey whole, using its triple rows of teeth, leaving no traces of its victims (including bones) behind.

Manananggal

The Manananggal of the Philippines has some features of the Penanggalan. This vampire is an old but attractive woman who preys on pregnant women and uses her tongue to suck the blood of their unborn babies. A child born with a deformed face is said to have been a victim. The Manananggal travels by separating at the waist. Her top half flies with bat wings while her bottom half remains behind. If you find the bottom half, you can destroy the manananggal by covering it with salt, garlic, or ashes.
